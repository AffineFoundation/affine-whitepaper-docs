# 7. Conclusion

Open-source reasoning progress has been held back not by a shortage of training capability but by the absence of a public mechanism that turns that capability into durable economic signal. Strong releases appear, get briefly recognized, and are absorbed back into whatever closed or fragmented system produced them. The increments that matter — real capability gains over what existed the week before — are not priced anywhere a participant can reliably collect on.

Affine is our attempt to supply that mechanism. Renewable, verifiable environments form the substrate that makes capability measurable in the first place. A permissionless network of miners trains against that substrate and releases measurably stronger models. A public scoring and settlement layer on Bittensor Subnet 120 prices those releases, rejects copies and noise, and converts verified increments into on-chain emissions that fund the next round of training. The loop is the point: each of the three pieces is the reason the others can stand.

What remains open is spelled out in [§6](06-roadmap.md) — harder surfaces, finer settlement resolution, deeper anti-exploit, permissionless participant roles, hardened infrastructure — and none of these are speculative. They are limits the system already knows it has, and the work from here is to keep pushing against them in the order that preserves settlement integrity.

The closing framing is the one that motivated the project from the start. Progress on open reasoning models should not be a property of a few closed labs that can afford large-scale training. It should be a property of a continuously-running public system: permissionless participants, transparent measurement, and economic incentives pointed at the thing that actually matters — better open-source models, week after week, released into the commons and paid for by the commons.
